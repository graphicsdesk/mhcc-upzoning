With Claremont Hall construction nearing completion, the MHCC rezoning proposal hopes to prevent similar real estate developments in the future

By Amira McKee and Esther Sun

September 27, 2022

Photo by Pippa Tsuki Carlson

At 100 Claremont Ave., a new 41-story residential tower dwarfs the gothic turrets of neighboring buildings such as the Union Theological Seminary and Riverside Church. Construction on Claremont Hall condominiums—whose website describes it as “infusing modern luxury with Gothic grandeur,” with listings ranging from $1.3 to $6.75 million—is nearing completion.

But as potential owners scope out the apartments, their continued construction is catalyzing other changes in the community.

Morningside Heights Community Coalition leaders fear the increase of private residential developments under current zoning policies poses a risk to affordable housing in the neighborhood. They cite Claremont Hall and the 33-story residential Vandewater at 543 W. 122nd St. as prime examples of mounting inaccessibility. Neither building includes any affordable housing units.

“The entire neighborhood [was] outraged that this was going to happen and we really had no say in it, that we couldn’t get any affordable housing out of it, that we couldn’t limit the height of the building,” Dave Robinson, MHCC President, said, referring to the Vandewater.

The Morningside Heights planning study, a rezoning proposal designed by the MHCC and released in May 2021, seeks to offer a path toward maintaining neighborhood affordability and character.

Formed in 2016, the MHCC held community meetings and consulted with city officials at the Department of City Planning to develop the rezoning framework.

“Proposals like this one are in the context of a … upzoning pressure citywide, at least under the de Blasio administration,” Aaron Passell, an urban sociologist and associate director of Columbia’s undergraduate urban studies program, said.

Passell added that the MHCC proposal’s community-based genesis and planning process makes it more suited to address the needs of “active members” in the community. It received support from current Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine when he served as the City Councilmember for District 7, and previous Manhattan Borough President Gale Brewer.

The Morningside Heights Planning Study proposes a combination of upzoning and downzoning potential sites of development. Upzoning raises the maximum number of floors a new building may have to encourage development. On the other hand, downzoning lowers the number of floors that may be built to protect rent-stabilized units from price increases.

The study also identified “soft sites”—locations at risk of luxury overdevelopment.

“We simply went around the neighborhood [and] pointed out things defined as a soft site, which is basically a … structure that is pretty much unoccupied or some storefronts, etc., that could easily be bought out,” Robert Stern, MHCC board member and leader of the rezoning study, said. “You’ll see that some of those sites actually have become the primary development sites for the rezoning.”

A presentation given at an MHCC rezoning meeting on April 4 identified four potential sites of upzoning: 568 W. 125th St., 111th to 112th streets and Broadway Avenue, 109th to 110th streets and Broadway Avenue, and 109th Street and Broadway Avenue.

In his presentation to the committee, Stern said one of the primary objectives of the rezoning study is to maximize affordable housing allotments within new developments.

On March 22, 2016, the New York City Council approved the mandatory inclusionary housing policy, which requires a percentage of units in new developments to be reserved for residents whose incomes are 40 percent of the area median income. Because Morningside Heights has not been rezoned in over 60 years, the policy has not applied to neighborhood developments like Claremont Hall and Vandewater. But if the study is passed, the rezoning it prescribes would activate affordable housing mandates within Morningside Heights.

“A higher percentage and deeper affordability, that’s part of what we hope to accomplish by getting this plan is to have a seat at the table, really, to help apply some kind of pressure on the developer,” Stern said.

The 109th Street and Broadway Avenue site, for example, is zoned to allow for 102 residential units, though there is currently no residential development. Of those units, zero are required to be affordable housing. After upzoning, as outlined in the planning study, this site could be developed with 150 units, 38 of which would be affordable.

However, Stern emphasized that rezoning itself will not necessarily lead directly to new buildings being constructed at the sites.

“I want to be clear: Just because we rezone certain areas, that doesn’t mean they’re going to be developed,” Stern said. “We’re just promoting reasonable development that will include affordable housing.”

Regardless of the prospects of eventual development, Passell believes that the city’s decision on the rezoning proposal will carry implications for similar community efforts in other parts of the city.

“There’s a set of network relationships among communities across the city. In a way, that means that if one accomplishes something, others are likely to learn from them,” Passell said. “So I would be very surprised if it weren’t the case that something similar would happen [with the MHCC]—that other neighborhood groups nearby wouldn’t see the successes of a group like this one and follow a similar process.”

Moving forward, the MHCC plans to continue campaigning to raise awareness of and support for the study in the community—with the hopes of receiving New York City Council approval in 2023.

“For the most part, I think it’s laudable for community organizing,” Passell said. “It’s also appropriate for the city to recognize the effort and the voice of people who have pulled together a proposal like this.”
